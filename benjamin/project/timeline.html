Timeline


1914

Assassination (June 28, 1914)

Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austrian throne is assassinated with his wife, in Sarajevo.

War Declaration (July 28 - August 1, 1914)

Austria-Hungary declares war on Serbia, and Germany declares war on Russia. France mobilizes, while Italy, Switzerland, Denmark, Sweden and Norway state their neutrality. Germany and Ottoman Empire create a secret alliance. The Schlieffen Plan is developed to allow Germany to successfully attack both fronts at the same time.

Invasion (August 4, 1914)

Germany invades Belgium to outflank the French army, and United Kingdom declares war on Germany, forcing Canada and Australia to join.

Battle of Liège (August 5 - 16, 1914)

German forces besiege and capture the fortresses of Liège. First battle in World War I. Start of trench warfare.

Battle of the Frontiers (August 7 - September 6, 1914)

A series of battles fought by Germany along the eastern frontiers of France against the British Expeditionary Force and France's Fifth Army. French offensives are pushed back.

Battle of Mulhouse (August 7 - 10, 1914)

The opening attack of the First World War by the French Army against Germany. The battle was part of a French attempt to recover the province of Alsace, which France had ceded to the new German Empire following defeat in 1870.

Campaign (August 9, 1914)

The British and French invasion of Togoland, a German colony in West Africa.

Battle of Lorraine (August 14 - 25, 1914)

The armies of France and Germany had completed their mobilisation, the French with Plan XVII, to conduct an offensive through Lorraine and Alsace into Germany and the Germans with Aufmarsch II West, for an offensive in the north through Luxembourg and Belgium into France, supplemented with attacks in the south to prevent the French from transferring troops to the greater threat in the north.

Battle of Stallupönen (August 17, 1914)

Opening battle of the eastern front, between Germany and Russia. Germany conducts a successful counterattack, pushing Russians out of east Prussia.

Battle of the Ardennes (August 21 - 23, 1914)

The German armies defeated the French and forced their retreat.

Battle of Tannenberg (August 23 - 30)

The battle resulted in the almost complete destruction of the Russian Second Army and the suicide of its commanding general, Alexander Samsonov. A series of follow-up battles destroyed most of the First Army as well and kept the Russians off balance until the spring of 1915. The battle is notable for fast rail movements by the German Eighth Army, enabling them to concentrate against each of the two Russian armies in turn.

Battle of Galicia (August 23 - September 11, 1914)

The Austro-Hungarian armies were severely defeated and forced out of Galicia, while the Russians captured Lemberg

Battle of Kraśnik (August 23 - 25, 1914)

The Austro-Hungarian First Army defeated the Russian Fourth Army. It was the first victory by Austria-Hungary in World War I. As a result, the First Army's commander, General Viktor Dankl, was lauded as a national hero for his success. The battle was also the first of a series of engagements between Austria-Hungary and Russia all along the Galicia front.

Siege of Maubeuge (August 24 - September 7, 1914)

The German bombardment assisted by agents in the Entrenched Position who passed reports on the fall of shot, greatly increasing the accuracy of the German guns. The forts and infantry shelters were wrecked by the German and Austrian super-heavy howitzers. Parts of Maubeuge were set on fire, causing civilians to evacuate. Maubeuge surrendered to the German army, effective at noon the next day

Battle of St. Quentin (August 29 - 30, 1914)

The Fifth Army attacked St. Quentin with their full force. The Germans captured orders from a French officer and General Karl von Bülow, commander of the German 2nd Army had time to prepare. The attacks against the town by the XVIII corps was a costly failure but X and III corps on the right were rallied by the commander of I Corps, General Louis Franchet d'Esperey. Advances on the right were made against Guise and forced the Germans, including the Guard Corps, to fall back.

First Battle of the Marne

German advance into France is halted, marking the failure of the Schlieffen Plan.

British Blockade (November 2, 1914)

British warships began a blockade, restricting goods from traveling into Germany, Austro-Hungary, and Turkey. This played an important role in stopping Germany from mass producing weapons and armoured vehicles.

First Battle of Champagne (December 20, 1914 – March 17, 1915)

The offensive was part of a French strategy to attack the Noyon Salient, a large bulge in the new Western Front, which ran from Switzerland to the North Sea. The First Battle of Artois began on the northern flank of the salient on 17 December and the offensive against the southern flank in Champagne began three days later. The battle ended with inconclusive results.

Christmas (December 24 - 25, 1914)

A Christmas truce is observed between German and British forces in the Western front.


1915

Battle of Bolimov (January 31, 1915)

First German use of chemical warfare that resulted in an unconclusive battle against Russians.

The Gallipoli Campaign (February 19, 1915)

The Gallipoli Campaign begins with a French and British naval attack on the Dardanelles.

The Second Battle of Ypres (April 22 - May 25, 1915)

Germany initiates the first poison gas warfare, using chlorine gas. The battle ends in a stalemate.

Alliance (April 26, 1915)

Italy signs the Treaty of London with the Entente.

Gorlice-Tarnów Offensive (May 1, 1915)

German offensive of Galicia to relieve pressure on Austro-Hungarians.

Lusitania (May 7, 1915)

The Lusitania, carrying over 170 tons of ammunition and 128 US citizens, was sank by a German U-boat, forcing USA to finally declare war 2 years later.

Italy (May 23, 1915)

The Kingdom of Italy declares war against Austria-Hungary.

Russian Great Retreat (June - September, 1915)

A strategic retreat by the Russians, giving up Galicia and Poland.

The August Offensive

A series of diversionary battles to try and break the Ottoman Empire's defensive lines. Ends unsuccessfully.

Second Battle of Champagne

French offensive against the German army at Champagne that coincided with an Anglo-French assault at north-east Artois and ended with French retreat.

Gallipoli Retreat (December 18, 1915)

Allied invasion of the Gallipoli peninsula is a failure. Major victory for the Ottomans.


1916

The Battle of Verdun (February 21, 1916)

German and British forces would continue to fight for 10 months, as it remained stalemated. First German use of the flamethrower, Wechselapparat.

Battle of Jutland (May 31, 1916)

Largest naval battle in the war, between Britain's Grand Fleet and Germany's Hochseeflotte.

Brusilov Offensive (June 4, 1916)

A major offensive in the Eastern front. Russia captures Galicia and diverts many German soldiers from Verdun. The cost of lives are high with over 1.4 million Russian casualties. This made Russia unable to launch any offensive of the same size.

The Battle of the Somme (July 1, 1916)

To force German troops elsewhere, the British army invaded Somme river, which would lead to over 1,000,000 casualties in under 5 months. This battle also introduced the first British Landship tanks into the war.


1917

Russian Revolution (March 1917)

The Russian Revolution makes Russia unable to participate in the war.

Battle of Vimy Ridge (April 9 - 12, 1917)

4 divisions of the Canadian Corps, captured Vimy Ridge, which would protect the British armies from German defensive fire. Supported by the artillery barrages, the Canadians would swiftly capture the ridge, and force the Germans to retreat.

Battle of Passchendaele (July 31 - November 10, 1917)

One of the bloodiest offensives in the war, only lasting 3 months. Passchendaele was known for being extremely muddy, with soldiers and horses drowning in the thick mud. It resulted in an allied victory, at a high cost of lives, but they were unsuccessful in reaching their main objective.

First Battle of Monte Grappa (November 11 - December 23, 1917)

With new defensive fortifications, the Italian army would stop the Austro-Hungarian Offensive, even with German numerical superiority.

The Battle of Cambrai (November 20 - December 3, 1917)

The British tanks push through German defenses but bad weather caused infantry reinforcements to be delayed, and two weeks later, they would be pushed back to their original defenses. Ends in a stalemate.

United States (December 7, 1917)

USA declares war against the Alliance.


1918

Kaiserschlacht (March 21 - April 5, 1918)

With Russia out of the war, Germany needed to destroy the Allies before United States reached the battlefield. With 50 free divisions from the Eastern Front, Germany would launch a full scale attack, composing of 4 offensives codenamed Michael, Georgette, Gneisenau, and Blücher-Yorck. Michael was the main attack, which was intended to break through the Allied lines, outflank the British forces and defeat the British Army. Once that was achieved, it was hoped that the French would seek armistice terms. The other offensives were subsidiary to Michael and were designed to divert Allied forces from the main offensive effort on the Somme. Despite their apparent successes, USA would land in France, starting the Hundred Day Offensive.

Hundred Days Offensive (August 8 - November 11, 1918)

These were a series of massive allied offensives against Germany, which would end the war. The United States had mobilized 5,000,000 soldiers, and with the Palestine Campaign complete, British soldiers were reinforced. The war on the Italian front had also been successful, bringing more troops to the final assault. Germany would soon be overwhelmed, retreating further and further into their captured territory.

Battle of Amiens (Hundred Days Offensive)

An allied offensive consisting of 10 allied divisions and 500 tanks, would rip through German defenses with minimal casualties . This attack was halted however, as German artillery and supplies were being moved to the sector. A fresh offensive would begin, and this time push the German army back 55 km.

Advance to the Hindenburg Line (Hundred Days Offensive)

With the front line broken, a number of battles took place as the Allies forced the Germans back to the Hindenburg Line. East of Amiens, with artillery brought forward and munitions replenished, the Fourth Army also resumed its advance, with the Australian Corps crossing the Somme River on the night of 31 August, breaking the German lines during the Battle of Mont Saint-Quentin. On 26 August, to the north of the Somme, the First Army widened the attack by another 11 km with the Second Battle of Arras of 1918, which includes the Battle of the Scarpe and the Battle of Drocourt-Queant Line. With the German Army deteriorated, the defenses would fall one by one.

The Meuse-Argonne Offensive (Hundred Days Offensive)

These were a series of final battles in the Western Front, involving mainly American and German soldiers. A last stand was made by the Germans in Argonne forest to prevent invasion of Germany. This battle was the bloodiest in all of American history, with over 125,000 casualties, and involved over 1,200,000 American soldiers. Due to their massive army, they were able to break the German formation and continue to Sedan.

Second Battle of Cambrai (Hundred Days Offensive)

The British army would break the Hindenburg Line, and have a clear passage to Germany. With its defensive positions all captured, Germany had no choice but to sign the Armistice.

Armistice (November 11, 1918, 11:00 AM)

These losses would force the German empire to sign an armistice or they would face invasion. Kaiser Wilhelm II would also abdicate his throne.
